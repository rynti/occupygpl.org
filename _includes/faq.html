Frequently Asked Questions

Q: I don’t want others to close my code! A: They can’t, your code still is open source. What did they close then? THEIR work which just happens to be based on your open source code. If you don’t like this, then your existing license may be a good fit after all.

Q: What happens if they write a wrapper around my lib and sell it for $10.000? A: Yes, that could happen but it’s also a rather unlikely scenario. If all they’ve done is a thin wrapper then you or someone else in the open source community is also capable of creating such a thin wrapper in no time. Then all you need to do is undercut them by $10.000 and a good chunk of freedom.

The more likely scenario is that a company takes your code and produces a large amount of other code that just happens to use your lib at its core. The said company will sell their code and their extensions for a large sum of money and they are perfectly entitled to do so. It’s after all THEIR code.

Even this scenario is beneficial to you. Said company will likely find bugs and fix them.

Q: Open source projects can’t live without the restrictions the GPL offers! A: This is not a question, and furthermore it’s not true! There’s a wide variety of popular open source projects that use permissive licenses: Clang, LLVM, Node.js, jQuery, just to name a few.

Q: What’s with the name? "Occupy GPL", do you want to destroy the GPL? And all GPL projects? A: No, even though it may sound like it (Especially thanks to the old really misleading subtitle). The name was chosen because it’s very aggressive and generates a lot of attention. The GPL isn’t as good a license as many believe and it really shouldn’t be used as much as it is today in open source software. Thats an opinion. There is lots of cool software licensed under the GPL that is used by many people every day: Linux, Git, Blender and a lot more. Kudos to all these awesome folks!

Q: You clearly have no idea what free software is about. A: Maybe, but I’m more interested in open source software anyway (The FSF makes a distinction here). I’m also not interested in politics. Just technology and how to improve it.
A nice quote from Linus Torvalds:

"That’s the point of open source – the ability to make the code better for your particular needs, whoever the ‘your’ in question happens to be."

Q: What about the LGPL? It seems to fit your problem. A: Although the LGPL is a huge improvement over the GPL and somewhat solves a lot of the problems mentioned above, it’s also way more complicated to use than a permissive license and you still have the risk of commiting copyright infringement just by using the project the wrong way.

Q: Isn’t this a bit too aggressive? This site and all? GPL is cool, please don’t hate it. A: Yes, it’s aggressive, but that was the intention ;) There’s a misconception out there that GPL would be the right choice for almost every open source project out there, and it requires the attention of the community in order to bust this myth. If you’re happy with everything as it is now, cool - have a nice day then! If, however, you notice a problem in a post like this one , you’ve come to the right place!

Q: What is the purpose of this site? A: This site attempts to encourage the usage of permissive open source licenses and raise awareness that the GPL isn’t the appropriate license for every open source project.

Q: I HATE YOU, I HATE THIS SITE, AND I’LL MAKE MY OWN OCCUPY PERMISSIVE LICENSES SITE NOW! A: Cool. Feel free to fork this page. You can even relicense it under GPL terms if you want to, it’s MIT licensed after all.
